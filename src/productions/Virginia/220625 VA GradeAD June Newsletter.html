Civil rights and liberties are under attack in Commonwealth. From school censorship to threats against LGBTQ+ students to the rollback of commonsense criminal legal reforms to attacks on reproductive freedom to barriers to voting – we're seeing it on all fronts and are keeping a watchful eye on what happens next.

Here's what you need to know about what's going on in Virginia right now:

Abortion rights and the fall of Roe v. Wade

The recent overturn of Roe v. Wade is a devastating blow to the rights of women and pregnant people in our country. But let us be clear: Abortion is safe and legal in Virginia – at least for now. It is up to each and every one of us to keep it that way so that Virginians and anyone who seeks abortion services in Virginia can do so free from government interference.

Keep up with what's happening and get involved in the fight for abortion rights in Virginia on our website.

School censorship and book banning

If you thought efforts to ban books and control what students read and learn was limited to schools, think again – now people are trying to sue private bookstores like Barnes and Noble to halt the sale of the books Gender Queer and A Court of Mist and Fury citing they are obscene.

We filed a case last week on behalf of several independent Virginia bookstores, the Authors Guild and Association of American Publishers that argues the case brought against the authors and publishers threatens the First Amendment rights of young people to choose to read First-Amendment-protected material, and the First Amendment rights of booksellers and publishers to curate and provide access to a variety of materials that present different perspectives and views. Read the press release.

Earned sentence credits

Last week, the General Assembly passed Gov. Youngkin's amendment to Virginia's budget that repealed expansions to the earned sentence credit program for Virginians who are incarcerated. Earned sentence credit allows someone to work towards their freedom by improving themselves and rehabilitating.

But this repeal means that this promise has been ripped away from hundreds of Virginians who earned their way home. Families will now have to wait longer to be reunited and people behind bars will lose more precious time to a carceral system from which they have already been deemed worthy of release. They kept their end of the bargain - too bad the state did not follow suit.
