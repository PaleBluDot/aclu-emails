Dear ##SM_RULE_360 Email Salutation##,

Bills 51 and 52 are up for a hearing again, after being deferred at the last hearing. We’re pleased the city council is taking a long, skeptical look at these bills, but they need to hear from you. Now we are asking for your written testimony again, and for those in Honolulu on Wednesday, August 29 at 1 p.m. to attend the hearing for an even more powerful community showing.

The idea that we can fix our housing crisis by making it a crime to not have a home is absurd. Our ‘ohana and neighbors who cannot afford rent are families, veterans, the medically frail, and even our keiki and kupuna. We need your help making sure the City Council and mayor know there is no aloha in punishing those in need because of their needs.

Criminalizing people for being poor is cruel, unconstitutional, and makes poverty worse. Ask the City Council to vote NO on Bills 51 and 52.

Our government must address the consequences of our housing crisis in a constitutional and humane manner. City Council Bills 51 (criminalizing “obstructing” a sidewalk) and 52 (criminalizing “lodging” on sidewalks) fail on both counts and should be voted down.

Bills 51 and 52 are not solutions to our housing crisis or its consequences. These bills will only increase the number of people who can’t afford to pay their rent, and will result in those people being harassed, arrested, fined, jailed, and saddled with a criminal record. There is nothing compassionate about adding to the already heavy burden of climbing out of poverty.

These continued efforts to sweep unsheltered people endangers civil rights, and is also financially irresponsible. It costs the city and state millions of dollars every year to “sweep” Hawai'i residents from one corner of the island to another and to store their personal belongings. It also costs the city hundreds of thousands of dollars to defend its unconstitutional war on the poor in court. We would rather see those dollars address the causes of our housing crisis. The money would be better spent on mental health programs, combatting substance abuse, and increasing the availability of permanent housing.

Please ask City Council to abandon the failed idea that sweeping human beings from place to place will somehow solve our housing crisis.

It is time to demand leadership and real solutions that give everyone the support they need to have a permanent home and be more self-sufficient.

Sincerely,

Joshua Wisch
Executive Director
ACLU of Hawai'i
