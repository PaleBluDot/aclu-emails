BLAST FROM THE PAST

On Criminal Justice, William Barr Is Stuck in the 90s

Almost five years to the day of the fatal police shooting of Michael Brown in Ferguson, Missouri, Attorney General William Barr delivered a major policy address in New Orleans. His speech dismissed police violence and espoused a dangerous vision for America – one in which 1990s-era tough-on-crime politics shape policies and pro-civil rights attitudes are derided as "anti-police." But this fear-mongering rhetoric no longer resonates with voters in the ways that it did in the 80s and 90s. Read more →

Comedian W. Kamau Bell on Making Sense of America

This week's podcast guest is W. Kamau Bell: standup comedian, prolific podcaster, and host of his own show on CNN, "United Shades of America." CASHLESS DYSTOPIA

Why Don't We Have More Privacy When We Use a Credit Card?

Simply put, cash is good for keeping people from prying into our lives, and credit cards are not. Credit card companies often get access to a lot of information about a given transaction, like how much we spend on travel, restaurants, political or religious donations, liquor stores, sex shops, and on and on. Given the state of our weak privacy laws, retailers and other actors can acquire that information, combine it with other data, and use it as they see fit. Read more →

By Jay Stanley

Say No to the 'Cashless Future' – and to Cashless Stores

The rise of cashless establishments is happening amid continuing hype over the supposed dawn of a "cashless future" and agitation by some very powerful interests that would love to see cash disappear. Credit card companies love it, as do many in the tech industry. This is a trend that should very much be nipped in the bud for a wide range of reasons. Thankfully, we're seeing a growing pushback against the supposed cashless future. Here's what you can do to resist a cashless world. Read more →

HOW NOT TO SOLVE THE GUN EPIDEMIC

Expanding Involuntary Confinement Is Not the Answer to Gun Violence

In the wake of recent tragic shootings in El Paso and Dayton, President Trump did what he does best: stoked fear and cast blame. He proclaimed that "we must reform our mental health laws to better identify mentally disturbed individuals who may commit acts of violence and make sure those people, not only get treatment, but when necessary, involuntary confinement." But there are two things wrong with the idea of involuntary confinement as a solution to gun violence. First, the data is clear: Mental disability is not the primary cause of gun violence. Second, making it easier to commit people against their will would rob innocent people of their most basic civil liberties. Read more →

By Susan Mizner and Ari Ne'eman
August 13, 2019
 
THE SUIT MUST GO ON

Federal Court Sounds Alarm on Face Recognition Technology

Last week, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit became the first appellate court in the nation to directly address the privacy harms posed by face recognition technology. The court ruled in a case brought by a group of Facebook users from Illinois, who allege that Facebook violated a state law by using face recognition technology on the users' photographs without their knowledge and consent. This ruling, which allows the case to move forward, explains why surreptitious use of face recognition by corporations harms people's privacy, and reaffirms the right to turn to the federal courts for redress. NEW ASSAULT ON TRANS RIGHTS

Opposition to Trump's Anti-Trans Health Care Plan Mounts

As part of its ongoing efforts to undermine access to health care for marginalized communities, the Trump administration is trying to weaken a section of the Affordable Care Act that protects transgender, non-binary, and gender nonconforming people, as well as people seeking, or who have obtained, services related to pregnancy, childbirth, and abortion. Yet try as it might, the administration can neither erase transgender people from existing statutory protections nor exclude them from society. Read more →
